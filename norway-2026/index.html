The shape of the trip

Oslo → Jotunheimen loop → Oslo

Two nights in Oslo to recover from the flight and buy fuel/food, then a 7-day loop out of Gjendesheim through the heart of Jotunheimen, then back to Oslo for the flight home. The whole thing is car-free: one coach in, one coach out, and a ferry across Lake Gjende. ~65/35 mountain/city — your target. In the mountains you're fully self-supported — camping every night under Norway's right-to-roam, with the DNT huts kept in your back pocket purely as a weather bailout.

why a loop Starting and finishing at the same trailhead (Gjendesheim) means one coach each way and zero taxis — and it's forced by the transport reality below. A point-to-point traverse would strand you with no September bus out of the mountains.

Getting there & around

The logistics that shape everything

the big one All the cheap seasonal feeder buses into Jotunheimen stop Aug 16, 2026 (Innlandstrafikk's official 2026 timetable) — the Otta→Gjendesheim, Lom→Spiterstulen, and Lom→Juvasshytta shuttles are done before you land. So the train-to-Otta plan is out.
the fix The direct Valdresekspressen coach Oslo ↔ Gjendesheim (NOR-WAY) runs into early October. Bus in, hike the loop, bus out. Done.

The route

The Jotunheimen loop

~62 mi over 7 hiking days, front-loading the epic stuff while you're fresh, then tapering through three moderate days to the exit. Two marquee days — Besseggen and Galdhøpiggen — are done on a day pack.

respect these two The Besseggen scramble (D1) is a ~1,300 ft hands-on grade-1 section — through-hiking E→W you descend it into Memurubu with full packs (the committing direction; take it slow, spot each other). The no-scramble Bessvatnet bypass skips it entirely if anyone's not feeling it. And we deliberately ferry out from Gjendebu to skip the sketchy chained Bukkelægret descent. Don't start exposed sections in fog or strong wind.
camp flexibility Since you're tenting, you're not tied to hut spacing — pitch wherever's scenic and break a long day in half if you want (e.g. camp near Russvatnet to split D2). The hut names below are your navigation waypoints and weather bailouts, not your bedtime.

Eating on the trail

Food & resupply — fully self-supported

You're camping and cooking every meal — completely self-supported. The stove + fuel (bought in Oslo) and one-pot meals mean you eat on your own schedule, wherever you pitch. Resupply at hut kiosks along the way so you never carry more than ~3 days of food at once.

The system

  • Carry ~3 days, resupply en route. Buy your start in Oslo, then restock at kiosks along the way — no hauling a full week of food.
  • Cook one-pot. Fast, cheap, warm, and entirely on your schedule.
  • Hot food without sleeping in. Passing a staffed hut at mealtime? Nothing stops you buying a meal, coffee, or a waffle — you're just not bunking there.
  • Camp is free. Under the right-to-roam your only mountain spend is food, fuel, ferry & coach.

Resupply points along the loop

  • Gjendesheim, Glitterheim, Leirvassbu, Gjendebu — staffed-hut kiosks / DNT provisions
  • Spiterstulen & Memurubu — private lodges with shops
  • Olavsbu — self-service provisions room; pay via the DNT app

Confirm stock & hours when booking — shoulder-season kiosk hours vary. Carry 1–2 emergency dinners as backup.

Lock it in

Booking checklist

no hut bookings Since you're camping, nothing in the mountains needs reserving — just transport, the Oslo apartment, and your weather-bailout kit.
  1. Flights — locked (Aug 25 / Sep 7).
  2. DNT Ung membership for at least 1–2 of you (~$36) + grab the DNT standard key — this is your storm escape hatch: it opens any locked self-service/emergency hut if the weather forces you off the tents. → dnt.no
  3. Valdresekspressen coach both ways (Oslo ↔ Gjendesheim) — nor-way.no / Entur once 2026 times publish.
  4. Gjende ferry — just the finale, Gjendebu→Gjendesheim (Sep 5) at gjende.no. Pre-book the early boat.
  5. Oslo apartment — one 2–3BR for Aug 26–28 and Sep 5–7 (same place if possible).
  6. Oslo sauna (KOK/SALT) for Sep 6 — book the slot now.

Huts are first-come if you ever need one — "no one is turned away in the mountains." Carry enough cash/card for an unplanned emergency hut night.

What to expect out there

Conditions & safety

  • Daylight: plenty (~14 h on Sep 1, sunrise ~06:15 / sunset ~20:40) but shrinking ~5 min/day — start early, carry headlamps.
  • Temps: valleys 45–52 °F high / 34–39 °F low; on the high passes and Galdhøpiggen subtract ~10–18 °F — frost up high, near-freezing in bad weather. Real shell + insulation + gloves/hat.
  • Rain ~44% of days. Plan for wet roughly every other day.
  • Snow can fall above ~4,900 ft anytime and hides cairns. Don't start exposed sections (Besseggen, Galdhøpiggen) in fog or strong wind.
  • Camping every night = committing to cold, possibly wet pitches this late. Bring a 3-season tent that handles wind, a sleeping bag rated to ~25 °F / -4 °C (or warmer), and an insulated pad (R-value ≥ 3.5). This is the gear that makes or breaks the trip.
  • The huts are your safety margin — staffed & open through ~Sep 13, so even though you're tenting, there's a warm bailout and kiosk resupply the whole route. If a real storm rolls in, duck into one (you've got the key). Summer footbridges still in.
  • Follow the Fjellvettreglene (Mountain Code); leave a route plan; it's never too late to turn around.

If you want to tweak it

Options & alternatives

Push past 65/35 (more mountain)

Skip the Aug 27 prep day, buy fuel the afternoon you land, coach out Aug 27, and add an 8th hiking day — an extra Glitterheim night with a Glittertind 8,090 ft summit, or a Memurubu night. Costs the jetlag buffer.

Gentler / cheaper (skip the big summit)

Drop the Glitterheim–Spiterstulen–Galdhøpiggen arm and do a relaxed western Gjende loop (Besseggen → Gjendebu → Olavsbu → Leirvassbu) with 5–8 mi days and tons of camping flexibility. No 4,850 ft climb, but you trade away the summit.

The glacier-guide Galdhøpiggen

Exists from Juvasshytta (roped glacier crossing, ~$50pp, daily through Sep 13) — but it forces a messier route + a private transfer out in September. The free Spiterstulen route in this plan gets the same summit cleaner.

Duck into huts more

Camping's the plan, but the huts are right there if the cold/wet wears thin — swap any night for a bed (~$15–30 youth-member at DNT huts, more at the private lodges). No need to decide in advance; pay on arrival. A warm dry night mid-trip can save morale.

Sanity check

The solo-hiker test

A YouTuber's "Solo Hiking 100 km in Jotunheimen" (Jun 2022, ~60 mi, 11,427 ft gain) runs almost exactly this terrain — independent confirmation we've put you on the right ground. The difference is pace: he crushed it solo in ~3 brutal days (~14 h moving daily, deep June snow); you do the same legendary loop over 7 humane days, add Galdhøpiggen (he skipped it), and have it in melted-out late-summer conditions. Like him you're self-supported and camping every night — just at a saner pace, with the huts as a storm bailout he didn't bother with.
